# Prokaryote to Eukaryote Transition

## Definition
The emergence of eukaryotic cells from prokaryotic ancestors—one of biology's most profound organizational leaps. Date: ~1.5-2.0 billion years ago.

## What Changed (Abstract)

### Before: Prokaryotes
- Simple, streamlined
- No compartments (diffusion-based transport)
- Small size (1-10 μm)
- Rapid reproduction

### After: Eukaryotes
- Complex, compartmentalized
- Active transport (cytoskeleton, motors)
- Large size (10-100 μm)
- Slow reproduction, but higher complexity

**Abstraction**: Traded **speed for complexity**, **simplicity for sophistication**.

## Three Key Innovations

### 1. Nuclear Envelope
**Principle**: **Separate information storage from protein synthesis**.

**Consequence**:
- **Transcription** (nucleus) and **translation** (cytoplasm) uncoupled
- **mRNA processing**: Splicing (introns removed), capping, polyadenylation
- **Regulatory opportunities**: Control gene expression at multiple levels
- **Alternative splicing**: One gene → multiple proteins

**Abstraction**: **Deferred execution**—mRNA edited before translation.

### 2. Endosymbiosis
**Principle**: **Symbiotic merger creates composite organism**.

See [[Endosymbiosis]] for full details.

**Mitochondria**:
- **Origin**: α-proteobacterium engulfed by archaeal host (~1.5-2.0 Gya)
- **Function**: Aerobic respiration (ATP production)
- **Evidence**: Own DNA (circular, bacterial), own ribosomes (70S), double membrane

**Chloroplasts** (plants, algae):
- **Origin**: Cyanobacterium engulfed (~1.0-1.5 Gya)
- **Function**: Photosynthesis
- **Secondary endosymbiosis**: Some protists acquired chloroplasts from other eukaryotes

**Energy scaling**:
- Mitochondria provide 10-100x more ATP per volume
- **Enabled**: Larger cell size, energetically expensive processes (active transport, transcription regulation)

### 3. Cytoskeleton
**Principle**: **Dynamic internal architecture**.

**Components**:
- **Microtubules**: Structural highways, mitotic spindle, flagella/cilia
- **Actin filaments**: Cell shape, motility, cytokinesis
- **Intermediate filaments**: Mechanical strength

**Consequence**:
- **Intracellular transport**: Kinesin, dynein motors walk along microtubules
- **Mitosis**: Chromosome segregation via spindle
- **Larger size**: Active transport overcomes diffusion limits
- **Phagocytosis**: Engulf food particles (enabled endosymbiosis)

## Evolutionary Scenario (Likely Path)

### Step 1: Archaeal Host
- **Ancestor**: Archaeon (see [[Archaea]])
- **Asgard archaea**: Recently discovered group, closest prokaryotic relatives of eukaryotes
- **Features**: Cytoskeleton-like proteins, membrane remodeling machinery

### Step 2: Endosymbiosis
- **Host engulfs α-proteobacterium** (phagocytosis or parasitism?)
- **Symbiosis stabilizes**: Bacterium provides ATP, host provides nutrients, protection
- **Gene transfer**: Many mitochondrial genes transferred to nucleus (thousands of genes)

**Result**: Mitochondrion as permanent organelle.

### Step 3: Nuclear Envelope
**Debate**: Before or after endosymbiosis?

**Hypothesis 1 (nucleus-first)**: Nuclear envelope evolved to protect DNA from reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced by aerobic metabolism.

**Hypothesis 2 (mitochondria-first)**: Mitochondria came first, nuclear envelope evolved later (perhaps from ER).

### Step 4: Compartmentalization Cascade
- Endomembrane system evolves (ER, Golgi, vesicles)
- Lysosomes, peroxisomes differentiate
- **Result**: Highly compartmentalized cell

## Why Did It Happen?

### Energy Hypothesis (Nick Lane)
**Claim**: Endosymbiosis solved an energy crisis.

- Prokaryotes limited by surface-area-to-volume ratio (genome scales with surface area for energy production)
- Mitochondria provide **internal energy source**, decoupling genome size from cell surface
- **Enabled**: Massive genome expansion, regulatory complexity

### Complexity Hypothesis
Eukaryotic complexity required coordinated innovations:
- Cytoskeleton for intracellular transport
- Nucleus for complex gene regulation
- Mitochondria for energy to run machinery

**Rare event**: Requires multiple simultaneous changes. Explains why eukaryogenesis happened **only once** (monophyletic origin).

## Consequences

### Size Increase
- **Prokaryotes**: 1-10 μm, ~1 fL volume
- **Eukaryotes**: 10-100 μm, ~1,000 fL volume
- **Enabled**: Multicellularity, complex morphology

### Complexity Increase
- **Genome size**: 1-10 Mb (prokaryotes) → 10-10,000 Mb (eukaryotes)
- **Gene number**: 1,000-10,000 (prokaryotes) → 5,000-50,000 (eukaryotes)
- **Regulatory complexity**: Chromatin remodeling, alternative splicing, ncRNAs

### Multicellularity Enabled
See [[Unicellular to Multicellular Transition]]:
- Eukaryotic toolkit (cell adhesion, differentiation, signaling) required for complex multicellularity
- Prokaryotes form biofilms, simple multicellular structures
- Only eukaryotes achieved **complex multicellularity** (animals, plants, fungi)

## Why Only Once?
Eukaryogenesis is **monophyletic** (all eukaryotes share a single common ancestor). Why didn't it happen multiple times?

**Hypotheses**:
1. **Improbable event**: Required rare combination of circumstances
2. **Oxygen requirement**: Needed O₂ from cyanobacterial photosynthesis (~2.4 Gya)
3. **Competitive exclusion**: Once eukaryotes arose, they filled niches, preventing repeat
