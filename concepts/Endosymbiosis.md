# Endosymbiosis

## Definition
A symbiotic relationship where one organism (**endosymbiont**) lives inside another (**host**), and the relationship becomes permanent and heritable. **Transformative merger** creating a new composite organism.

## Core Principle
**Abstraction**: **1 + 1 = 1 (new)**.

Two independent organisms fuse into single entity with emergent properties neither possessed alone.

## Canonical Example: Eukaryotic Origin

### Mitochondria
**What happened**: Ancestral eukaryote (archaeon) engulfed α-proteobacterium (~1.5-2.0 billion years ago).

**Evidence**:
1. **Own DNA**: Circular chromosome (bacterial-style), ~16 kb in humans
2. **Own ribosomes**: 70S (prokaryotic), not 80S (eukaryotic)
3. **Double membrane**: Inner = bacterial membrane, outer = host phagosome
4. **Phylogeny**: Mitochondrial genes cluster with α-proteobacteria
5. **Division**: Binary fission (like bacteria), not synchronized with cell division

**Consequence**: **10-100x ATP production** → enabled eukaryotic complexity.

### Chloroplasts (Plants, Algae)
**What happened**: Eukaryote engulfed cyanobacterium (~1.0-1.5 Gya).

**Evidence**:
1. **Own DNA**: Circular, ~120-200 kb
2. **Own ribosomes**: 70S (prokaryotic)
3. **Double membrane**: (Triple in some algae—secondary endosymbiosis)
4. **Thylakoids**: Internal membrane system (photosynthesis)
5. **Phylogeny**: Chloroplast genes cluster with cyanobacteria

**Consequence**: **Photosynthesis** → plants, oxygen production.

### Secondary Endosymbiosis
Some protists acquired chloroplasts by **engulfing eukaryotic algae**:
- **Diatoms, brown algae**: Red algal endosymbiont
- **Euglenids**: Green algal endosymbiont
- **Result**: Triple/quadruple membranes (nested endosymbiosis)

## Lynn Margulis (1967)
**Revolutionary proposal**: Endosymbiotic origin of mitochondria, chloroplasts.

**Initially rejected**: Dismissed as radical speculation.

**Now accepted**: Overwhelming molecular, phylogenetic evidence confirms theory.

**Quote**: *"Life did not take over the globe by combat, but by networking."*

## The Evolutionary Process

### Stage 1: Engulfment
- Host cell engulfs bacterium via phagocytosis
- Bacterium survives inside host (not digested)

### Stage 2: Mutual Benefit
- **Endosymbiont**: Provides ATP (mitochondria) or sugars (chloroplasts)
- **Host**: Provides nutrients, protection
- **Symbiosis stabilizes**: Both benefit (mutualism)

### Stage 3: Genomic Integration
- **Gene transfer**: Endosymbiont genes transferred to host nucleus (thousands of genes)
- **Protein import**: Nucleus-encoded proteins imported back to organelle
- **Dependency**: Organelle can no longer survive independently

### Stage 4: Vertical Transmission
- Organelles passed from mother cell to daughters (vertical inheritance)
- **Organelle = permanent feature** of eukaryotic cell

## Why Did It Succeed?

### Energy Hypothesis (Nick Lane)
- **Prokaryotes**: Energy production limited by surface area (respiratory chain on membrane)
- **Eukaryotes**: Mitochondria provide internal energy source
- **Consequence**: Genome size, complexity no longer constrained by cell surface

### Symbiotic Advantage
- **Division of labor**: Host manages cell structure, endosymbiont manages energy
- **Specialization**: Each partner optimizes distinct function
- **Result**: More efficient than either alone

## Other Examples of Endosymbiosis

### Coral-Zooxanthellae
- Corals host photosynthetic dinoflagellates
- **Benefit**: Zooxanthellae provide sugars, coral provides nutrients
- **Climate change**: Bleaching = expulsion of zooxanthellae (stress)

### Legume-Rhizobia
- Plants host nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules
- **Benefit**: Bacteria fix N₂ → NH₃, plant provides sugars
- **Result**: Legumes thrive in nitrogen-poor soils

### Termite Gut Symbionts
- Termites host protists/bacteria that digest cellulose
- **Benefit**: Termites can eat wood (can't digest cellulose alone)

### Lichen
- Fungus + alga (or cyanobacterium)
- **Benefit**: Fungus provides structure, alga provides photosynthesis
- **Result**: Colonize bare rock, extreme environments

## Abstraction: Levels of Integration

### Level 1: Facultative Symbiosis
- Partners can survive independently
- **Example**: Squid-Vibrio (bioluminescent bacteria)

### Level 2: Obligate Symbiosis
- Partners cannot survive alone
- But retain separate reproduction
- **Example**: Coral-zooxanthellae

### Level 3: Organellar Integration (Endosymbiosis)
- Complete dependency
- Vertical transmission (organelles inherited)
- **Example**: Mitochondria, chloroplasts

**Endosymbiosis = deepest integration**.

## Evolutionary Significance

### Enabled Eukaryotic Complexity
See [[Prokaryote to Eukaryote Transition]]:
- Mitochondria → energy scaling
- Nucleus + mitochondria → eukaryotic cell plan
- **Consequence**: Multicellularity, complex organisms possible

### Rare Event
Endosymbiosis (organellar level) is **exceedingly rare**:
- Mitochondria: **Once** in all of life (all eukaryotes monophyletic)
- Chloroplasts: **Once** (primary), secondary events rare
- **Why rare?**: Requires precise conditions, genetic/metabolic compatibility
