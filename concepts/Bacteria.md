# Bacteria

## Definition
One of three domains of life. Prokaryotic, single-celled organisms lacking nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. The most ancient, numerically dominant, and metabolically diverse form of life.

## Key Organizational Features

### Prokaryotic Architecture
- **No nucleus**: DNA in nucleoid region (not membrane-enclosed)
- **No organelles**: No mitochondria, ER, Golgi (eukaryotic compartments absent)
- **Ribosomes**: 70S (smaller than eukaryotic 80S)
- **Cell wall**: Peptidoglycan (structural support, osmotic protection)

### Genome Organization
- **Circular chromosome**: Single circular DNA molecule (typically 1-10 Mb)
- **Plasmids**: Extrachromosomal DNA (often carry antibiotic resistance, virulence factors)
- **Compact**: High gene density, minimal intergenic regions, no introns (mostly)

### Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT)
**Key abstraction**: Genetic information flows **laterally**, not just vertically (parent → offspring).

**Mechanisms**:
- **Transformation**: Uptake of naked DNA from environment
- **Conjugation**: Direct cell-to-cell transfer via pilus
- **Transduction**: Viral-mediated transfer

**Consequence**: Bacterial evolution is **networked**, not strictly tree-like. Phylogenies are complex.

## Metabolic Diversity
Bacteria occupy every thermodynamic niche:

### Energy Sources
- **Phototrophs**: Light → energy (cyanobacteria, purple bacteria)
- **Chemotrophs**: Chemical bonds → energy
  - **Chemoorganotrophs**: Organic molecules (most bacteria)
  - **Chemolithotrophs**: Inorganic molecules (sulfur, iron, ammonia oxidizers)

### Carbon Sources
- **Autotrophs**: CO₂ fixation (cyanobacteria, chemoautotrophs)
- **Heterotrophs**: Organic carbon (most bacteria)

### Electron Acceptors
- **Aerobic**: O₂ (most efficient)
- **Anaerobic**: NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₂, Fe³⁺ (diverse)
- **Fermentation**: Organic molecules

**Result**: Bacteria colonize extreme environments (hot springs, deep ocean, soil, animal guts).

## Rapid Evolution
- **Short generation time**: Minutes to hours
- **Large populations**: 10⁹ cells per gram of soil
- **High mutation rate**: ~10⁻⁹ per base per generation
- **HGT**: Acquire adaptive genes from distant lineages

**Consequence**: Rapid adaptation (antibiotic resistance evolves within years).

## Ecological Roles
- **Nitrogen fixation**: Convert N₂ → NH₃ (only bacteria/archaea)
- **Decomposition**: Recycle organic matter
- **Symbiosis**: Gut microbiome, legume root nodules
- **Pathogens**: Cholera, tuberculosis, plague, Lyme disease

## Abstract Principles

### Minimalism
Bacteria are **streamlined**:
- Small genome, minimal regulatory overhead
- No compartmentalization (diffusion sufficient)
- Rapid response to environment (transcription-translation coupled)

### Modularity
Horizontal gene transfer creates **modular evolution**:
- Operons: Functionally related genes clustered
- Plasmids: Mobile genetic cassettes
- **Result**: Mix-and-match genetic modules

### Network Phylogeny
Unlike eukaryotes (tree-like), bacteria have **reticulated phylogeny** due to HGT. The tree of life is more like a tangled web at bacterial level.

## Contrast with Eukaryotes
| Feature | Bacteria | Eukarya |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Nucleus | Absent | Present |
| Organelles | Absent | Present (mitochondria, etc.) |
| Genome | Circular, compact | Linear, introns, larger |
| Gene transfer | Horizontal + vertical | Mostly vertical |
| Complexity | Simple, streamlined | Complex, compartmentalized |
