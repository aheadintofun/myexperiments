# Archaea

## Definition
One of three domains of life. Prokaryotic like [[Bacteria]], but genetically and biochemically distinct. Often inhabit extreme environments, though also common in oceans and soil.

## Discovery
- **1977**: Carl Woese and George Fox proposed Archaea as separate domain based on ribosomal RNA (16S rRNA) sequences
- **Revolution**: Overturned prokaryote/eukaryote dichotomy → three domains (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya)
- **Key insight**: Archaea more closely related to Eukarya than to Bacteria (based on molecular phylogeny)

## Prokaryotic, But Distinct

### Shared with Bacteria (Prokaryotic Features)
- **No nucleus**: Nucleoid region, not membrane-bound
- **No organelles**: No mitochondria, ER, Golgi
- **Circular chromosome**: Compact genome
- **Small size**: Typically 1-10 μm

### Distinct from Bacteria

#### Membrane Chemistry
- **Ether lipids** (Archaea) vs. **ester lipids** (Bacteria/Eukarya)
- **Isoprenoid chains** vs. fatty acids
- **Monolayer option**: Some archaea have tetraether lipids forming monolayer membranes (unique)
- **Consequence**: Extreme stability (thermophiles, halophiles)

#### Cell Wall
- **No peptidoglycan** (unlike Bacteria)
- Pseudopeptidoglycan, S-layer proteins, or polysaccharides
- **Consequence**: Resistant to lysozyme, β-lactam antibiotics

#### Molecular Machinery
- **Transcription/translation**: More eukaryote-like than bacterial
- **RNA polymerase**: Multi-subunit, similar to eukaryotic RNA Pol II
- **Histones**: DNA-binding proteins (like eukaryotes, unlike bacteria)
- **Ribosome**: 70S, but protein composition differs from bacteria

## Extremophiles
Archaea thrive in extreme environments:

### Thermophiles
- **Pyrococcus furiosus**: Optimal growth at 100°C
- **Strain 121**: Grows at 121°C (autoclaving temperature)
- **Mechanism**: Heat-stable proteins, lipid monolayers

### Halophiles
- **Halobacterium**: Grows in saturated salt (30% NaCl)
- **Mechanism**: High intracellular K⁺ concentration, acidic proteins

### Methanogens
- **Methanogenesis**: Reduce CO₂ → CH₄ (methane)
- **Anaerobic**: Obligate anaerobes
- **Ecological role**: Rumen, wetlands, deep ocean (major methane source)

### Acidophiles/Alkaliphiles
- **Thermoplasma**: pH 2, 60°C (acidic hot springs)
- **Natronobacterium**: pH 10+ (soda lakes)

## Not Just Extremophiles
- **Marine archaea**: Abundant in oceans (Thaumarchaeota)
- **Soil archaea**: Common in soils
- **Human microbiome**: Present in gut, skin (though less than bacteria)

## Evolutionary Significance

### Closer to Eukaryotes
Molecular phylogeny indicates:
- Archaea + Eukarya share more recent common ancestor than either does with Bacteria
- **Eocyte hypothesis**: Eukaryotes arose from within Archaea (Asgard archaea as closest relatives)

### Eukaryogenesis Connection
See [[Prokaryote to Eukaryote Transition]]:
- **Hypothesis**: Eukaryotic nucleus derived from archaeal ancestor
- **Mitochondria**: Bacterial endosymbiont (α-proteobacterium)
- **Chimeric origin**: Eukaryotes = Archaea (host) + Bacteria (mitochondria)

## Metabolic Diversity (Different from Bacteria)
- **Methanogenesis**: Unique to Archaea
- **Ammonia oxidation**: Ammonia-oxidizing archaea (AOA) in oceans, soil
- **Limited pathogenicity**: No confirmed archaeal human pathogens (unlike bacteria)

## Abstract Principles

### Convergent Simplicity
Archaea and Bacteria independently evolved prokaryotic organization:
- **Same abstraction**: Minimalist, no compartmentalization
- **Different implementation**: Distinct membrane chemistry, molecular machinery

### Transitional Forms
Archaea exhibit features intermediate between Bacteria and Eukarya:
- **Information processing**: Eukaryote-like (transcription, translation)
- **Cell structure**: Prokaryotic (no nucleus, no organelles)
- **Metabolism**: Unique niches
